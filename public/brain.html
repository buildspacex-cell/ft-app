Foundation

The problem we are solving

Most retail investors in India own stocks they cannot explain. They bought based on a tip, a chart, or a feeling. They check the price every day but they cannot name the one reason they own the stock — and they would not know if that reason changed overnight.

"Why did I buy this in the first place?" — the question every retail investor asks at 7am when a headline drops about a stock they own.

The two moments we intercept

Pre-buy
The FOMO buy
WhatsApp tip, finfluencer reel, colleague who "just put in 2 lakh" — buying without answering 5 questions
Post-buy
The 7am panic
News drops, stock glows amber, brain floods with noise — no framework to know if the reason broke

The insight

The problem is not lack of information. It is too much information with no way to filter it against what actually matters to a specific investor's specific holding. A defence sector headline is noise to someone who owns HDFC Bank for its loan quality. It is signal to someone who owns a defence electronics company for India's localization push. FT knows the difference because it knows your reason.

Product

What Fundamentally True does

FT answers 5 questions about any NSE stock in plain English. Then it watches those answers every morning and finds you — only when something touches your specific reason for owning it.

The core loop

Step 1 — Understand once
5 questions answered
Plain English. What it does, how it makes money, can it be trusted, is the price fair, your one reason.
Step 2 — Save your reason
Thesis builder
Pick the reasons you believe. We track those specifically — not the general news.
Step 3 — We watch
7am morning card
Only arrives when something touches your reason. Quiet days are a feature, not a bug.

What the morning card contains

  • → The headline in plain English — no jargon
  • → Which of your saved reasons it touches
  • → Whether the reason is holding, wobbling, or broken
  • → Price vs story — did the price react ahead of the proof
  • → The calm read — what you actually need to do (usually: nothing)
  • → One plain-English term explained — something you heard today
  • → Feedback chips — was this useful / noise / already knew

The key design decisions

Silence as a feature. Every other fintech app optimises for engagement — more alerts, more reasons to open. FT optimises for peace of mind. If nothing touched your reason, we do not send a card. That silence is the product working correctly.

Reason-based filtering, not topic-based. We do not track "defence sector news." We track "India's defence electronics localization keeps growing" — a specific, falsifiable claim. The difference means we only surface news that could prove or disprove your specific bet.

Plain English as a hard constraint. Every output must be explainable to someone who does not follow finance. No jargon without a tooltip. No recommendation without a reason. "Buy" and "sell" never appear.

The Framework

The 5 questions

The entire product is built around these 5 questions. They are the campaign. They are the sample page. They are the morning card. Q5 is the product.

Q1
What does this company actually do?
Not the sector. The actual business. The shop story.
Q2
How does it make money — and is the money actually arriving?
Revenue mix, rhythm, cash following profit.
Q3
Why would this company still matter in 5 years?
Structural tailwind, moat, honest risk.
Q4
Is the price fair — or is the story already priced in?
Price vs history, price vs peers, what execution the price demands.
Q5
What is your one reason for owning this — and will you know if it changes?
The bridge question. This is the product. Pre-buy: what will your reason be? Post-buy: is it still true?

Most people can answer Q1. Almost nobody can answer Q5.

LLM Pipeline

How the digest is generated

Key prompt rules (src/lib/llm/prompts.ts)

  • → Split-signal rule — separate the headline (what happened) from the implication (what it means for your reason)
  • → Earnings-day rule — always explain whether results beat or missed expectations, not just the raw numbers
  • → Regulator vocabulary — use RBI, SEBI, TRAI correctly; never generic "regulator"
  • → Dual-currency rule — show both absolute (Rs X crore) and relative (% of revenue)
  • → Plain English rule — use [[TERM|explanation]] syntax for any jargon; JargonText component renders inline tooltips
  • → No buy/sell — never recommend action; only explain state change

The voice system prompt — core rules

  • → The shop-on-the-street rule — every business described as if it were a shop you walk past on MG Road or Brigade Road. "HDFC Bank runs 8,000 branches — the kind with a queue outside on salary day."
  • → Numbers always paired — "Net NPA of 0.3% — of every Rs 100 they lent out, less than 30 paise went bad."
  • → Status vocabulary locked — "holding" / "wobbling" / "broken". Never "consider trimming" or "reduce exposure".
  • → Weight vocabulary — alarm (reason broke), heads-up (upcoming event), tailwind (mild positive), quiet (low urgency)
  • → 8 few-shot examples baked into the prompt — HDFC NPA, TCS BFSI, Maruti dealer, RBI repo rate, Zomato acquisition, Jio pricing, Bajaj Finance digital, Sun Pharma US specialty
  • → Off the hook close — every digest ends with context that lets the reader know they don't need to do anything with this information

JargonText component

Parses [[TERM|explanation]] tokens in digest text. Renders coral-underlined tappable tooltips inline. Lives at src/components/digest/JargonText.tsx.

Product Spec

Q4 — Price analysis framework

The question "Is the price fair?" needs six distinct lenses. The current sample page shows lenses 1 and 2 only. This is the full spec for what Q4 should cover when the product is fully built.

Current state: gradient meter with 3 anchors (5yr avg, sector peers, today). Missing: growth-adjusted valuation, story vs price divergence, execution demand in plain English, time horizon calibration.

Lens 1 — Historical valuation range

Where does today's P/E, P/S, and EV/EBITDA sit versus the stock's own 3-5 year range? Is it at the expensive end of its own history or the cheap end? This anchors the price against the company's own past — independent of what the sector is doing.

Metrics
P/E, P/S, EV/EBITDA
Shown on a range bar: 3yr low → median → 3yr high → today
Plain English
"Expensive end of own history"
"The last time it was this expensive was during the 2021 bull run. It corrected 35% after."

Lens 2 — Sector peer comparison

Same metrics versus direct competitors — not the broad market. A defence electronics company is compared to other defence electronics companies. A private sector bank is compared to other private sector banks. Sector re-ratings matter: if the whole sector has re-rated upward, being expensive vs your own history may simply mean the sector premium has expanded.

Key insight from short-3: if the entire defence sector has re-rated, Data Patterns being expensive vs its own history may just be a sector-wide shift — not a company-specific bubble. The peer comparison separates these two explanations.

Lens 3 — Growth-adjusted valuation (PEG)

A stock at 40x earnings looks expensive until you see it growing at 45%. PEG ratio (P/E divided by earnings growth rate) gives context. Below 1 is generally considered fair for a growing company. Above 2 is demanding. This lens prevents the mistake of calling a fast grower expensive just because its P/E is high.

Calculation
PEG = P/E / EPS growth %
Use forward estimates, not trailing
Plain English read
"Fair for the growth rate"
"At 38x earnings with 40% earnings growth, you are paying roughly in line with what you are getting."

Lens 4 — Story vs price divergence

Has the price moved ahead of the actual evidence? This is tracked as the gap between price movement and proof. A defence sector headline may support the theme for Data Patterns — but if Data Patterns is not directly named in the order, the price moving 4% on that headline is the market pricing in something that has not been confirmed yet. That gap is a specific signal worth calling out.

Signal
Price moved on theme
Story has not confirmed yet. Worth watching — not worth panicking.
Signal
Story confirmed, price flat
Market has not priced it in yet. The reason got stronger without the price reflecting it.

This is already implemented in the morning card as "Price vs Story" — it shows the price change since last quarter alongside the reason status. The Q4 chapter should connect explicitly to this.

Lens 5 — What execution the price demands

This is the most useful lens for a retail investor. Convert the valuation into a plain English expectation. At the current price, what does the market need to happen for the price to be justified? This is reverse DCF thinking without the jargon.

Example: "At this price, the market is assuming revenue grows at 28% a year for the next 4 years with margins holding above 30%. The last 3 years averaged 31% revenue growth and 34% margins — so this is achievable but leaves no room for a slow quarter."

The plain English version: "The price is not wrong — but it leaves very little forgiveness. One missed quarter and the price has to find a new level."

Lens 6 — Time horizon calibration

A stretched valuation over 12-18 months is a real risk. The same valuation over 5 years may be irrelevant if the business keeps compounding. Price analysis without time horizon is incomplete. FT should ask: what is your horizon? And then qualify every price read against it.

5+ year horizon
Valuation premium may compress naturally
If the business keeps compounding, today's expensive price becomes tomorrow's cheap entry.
12-18 month horizon
Stretched valuation is a real risk
The market can stay wrong about price for years — but 12-18 months is not long enough to wait it out.

Product Idea — High Priority

The technical exit framework

When the reason is intact but the price has run ahead of the proof, FT watches the technical structure for the user. When the market starts correcting the overvaluation — first break, confirmed by volume — FT tells them. Until then: silence.

"Let it run. We keep watching — the story and the price — so you don't have to. One message when something is actually worth your attention."

The problem this solves

The most common and painful investor experience: own something good, it has run 50-60%, fundamentals still intact, but uneasy about the valuation. Current options are all bad:

  • ✗ Sell now on price anxiety — often premature, they sell and watch it go up another 30%
  • ✗ Hold and do nothing — no framework, pure hope, creates daily anxiety
  • ✗ Average down if it corrects — no system, just gut reaction
  • ✓ Stay in until the technical structure breaks, then exit with confirmation — this is what FT enables

The three-signal framework

FT checks three things simultaneously once a user is in a "reason intact, price stretched" situation:

Signal 1 — Fundamental
Did the reason change?
Already built. Tracked every morning. Reason breaks → fundamental exit signal regardless of price or technical structure.
Signal 2 — Valuation
Has price run ahead of proof?
Price vs story divergence. Partially built in morning card. Needs to connect explicitly to Q4 chapter and user's time horizon.
Signal 3 — Technical
Has the structure broken?
Not yet built. Key support level + volume confirmation. Only triggers after Signal 2 is active (price already stretched).

Critical design rule: Signal 3 only activates when Signal 2 is already true. A technical break during a market-wide correction that has nothing to do with the fundamental reason should not trigger the alert. Standard charting tools cannot make this distinction. FT can — and this is the moat.

The proprietary algo — what to build

Do not use standard RSI/MACD/moving averages. Those are available everywhere and add nothing. Build a system that combines:

  • → Support level identification — recent swing lows, volume-weighted price floors, previous breakout levels. Set relative to the stock's own structure, not arbitrary percentages.
  • → Volume confirmation — a price break on below-average volume is noise. A price break on 2x+ average volume is the market making a statement. Only the latter triggers the alert.
  • → Volatility calibration — a 3% move means nothing for a volatile mid-cap but is significant for a stable large-cap. Thresholds set relative to the stock's own historical volatility (ATR).
  • → Trend structure check — is the stock in an uptrend (higher highs, higher lows)? A break below the most recent higher low is the technical definition of "uptrend broken." Simple. Unambiguous.
  • → Fundamental filter — only flag if the technical break is NOT during a broad market correction. If Nifty is down 3% and the stock is down 4%, that is market noise. If Nifty is flat and the stock is down 4% on volume — that is a signal.

The morning card UX — watching levels

When a user is in "reason intact, price stretched" mode, the morning card gets a new persistent element: the watching levels bar. It appears every morning regardless of whether there is news — which is itself a signal (silence = structure still holding).

WATCHING — PRICE STRUCTURE
KEY SUPPORT — ₹1,680
Recent swing low · 14-day avg volume baseline
HOLDING
Today: ₹1,847 · Support at ₹1,680 · Distance: +9.9%
Volume: 0.8x average — below threshold for a break signal
Structure intact. If this closes below ₹1,680 on 2x+ average volume, we will find you immediately. Until then — reason holding, structure holding.

The morning card has three possible states for this block:

StateWhat it showsWhat it means
Structure holding "₹1,680 support. Distance: +9.9%. Volume: 0.8x. Holding." Nothing to do. Go back to sleep. Card appears but requires no action.
Structure testing "Momentum softening. Watching closely today." Not a turn yet. Early signal only. No action implied.
Structure broken "Price momentum has turned. Three signals checked — momentum now negative." Not a recommendation to sell. A notification that the run has changed character. The user decides what to do with that information.

What FT never says

FT never says "sell." FT says "price momentum has turned." The distinction is the entire brand. A momentum shift is a fact — observable, neutral, uncharged. What the user does with it is entirely their decision. This keeps FT on the right side of SEBI, on the right side of the brand promise, and — most importantly — on the right side of the investor's autonomy. The product's job is to make sure the user is never surprised. Not to tell them what to do.

Why this is a moat, not just a feature

  • → No screener can do this — Tickertape and Screener show technical indicators but cannot connect them to a specific user's saved reason and valuation context
  • → No finfluencer can do this — they tell you what to do, they don't watch your specific setup and alert you when something changes
  • → Creates daily engagement without anxiety — "structure holding" is a calming daily confirmation, not a fear-driven alert. Different emotional register entirely.
  • → Retention driver — "Let it run. We keep watching — the story and the price — so you don't have to." is the product promise in plain English. No conditions. No jargon. The user delegates the watching entirely. Confidence-driven engagement, not anxiety-driven.
  • → Proprietary data flywheel — as users interact with break alerts (did they act? did the stock recover?), FT learns which break signals are reliable for which stock types. The algo improves with usage.

The road to 2,000 early-access signups

A single, focused goal: 2,000 verified email signups for the June 24th first cohort. This plan works backwards from that number — what each asset must do, what each channel can realistically deliver, and what we build or fix before spending a rupee or an hour on distribution.

Target: 2,000 signups by June 24th. Working window: ~4 weeks. Required run-rate: roughly 70-80 signups per day on average, front-loaded lighter and back-loaded heavier as content compounds.

The conversion math

Every plan that hits a number is really a plan about a funnel. Here is the funnel we are solving for, with conservative assumptions. If the real rates beat these, we hit the goal earlier; if they lag, we know exactly which stage to fix.

Funnel stageAssumed rateVolume needed for 2,000
Content views (Reels, posts, forwards)—~200,000 views
View → landing page visit2.5%~20,000 visits
Landing visit → sample page / engaged35%~7,000 engaged
Engaged → signup28%~2,000 signups

The two numbers we do not control are total views and the view-to-visit rate. The two we fully control are landing engagement and signup conversion. That is why asset readiness comes first — a 28% engaged-to-signup rate is only achievable if the landing page and sample page are doing their job.

What we measure

One dashboard, checked daily. If a number lags, the calendar bends toward fixing it.

  • → Daily signups — the only number that matters. Tracked against the run-rate to 2,000.
  • → Source attribution — which channel and which short drives each signup (already captured in the source column).
  • → View-to-visit rate per short — tells us which creative to double down on.
  • → Sample completion rate — if people open the sample but do not finish, panel content needs work.
  • → Engaged-to-signup rate — if this lags 28%, the landing page or the offer needs sharpening, not more traffic.
  • → Stock interest answers — free-text stock captures double as a content goldmine: the most-named stocks become the next shorts and sample pages.
